Our journey starts with your message. Your message should state what you are offering, why you're offering it and who benefits from it. It is relaying the value of your business's products or services in a way that best appeals to the potential customer.

"You don't need more content. You need a clearer message." — Unknown

How do you know it's the right message?

It's all about what you say and how you say it. Above all, it should tell the people who are most likely to buy your product or service why they should care about it.

Think about a product or service that you wouldn't buy. Why wouldn't you buy it? Maybe because it's not something that matters to you. In Mason's dog washing business, he's not going to share the message about his Magic Suds with someone with no dog, but he would to someone with a dog. And, after learning about his customers' dog's needs, he might tell them all about why his shampoo is their answer.

That's how you determine that your message is right. It must appeal to the people who are most likely to buy your product, and tell how it meets their needs.

To make sure your message is the RIGHT one, the message should be:

✓
Clear and Concise
Your message should be to the point, and should not be confusing.
✓
Compelling
Your impact of your message should be powerful.
✓
Center the Solution
Your message should tell your customer how it solves their problem.

It's now your turn to make your own message!

Remember: A good message speaks directly to the right person.

To be successful, businesses must identify a specific audience or market based on shared traits, needs, and behaviors.

Now that you understand the mechanics of the message, we need to find out who to give that message to.

The people you are trying to reach are called your market. These are the people you want to reach because they are people who are most likely to purchase your product or service.

We mentioned earlier that Mason shouldn't tell people who didn't have a dog his message about his Magic Suds. That's because they would have no need for a shampoo that is made for a dog. Simple enough.

But what about what dog owners?

Should all dog owners get the message about the Magic Suds that help keep dog hair detangled?

We may need to do some market research to better understand who the message should go to.

🎯

Your target market = the people most likely to buy your product. Not everyone — just the right ones!

So Mason's got a target market to deliver his message to. Because he helps groom customers with all kinds of dogs, if the shampoo is for easy detangling, it should not go to people who have dogs with short hair. Why? They probably won't see much value in that product, and it probably won't fulfill a need. You want to direct your message to the people who are most likely to buy your product or service, so in Mason's case, only dog owners who have long haired dogs makes sense.

Performing market research is not all about researching your customer and their needs; it is also about researching your industry. An industry is a group of businesses that produce similar goods, services, or related economic activities. For instance, a doctor is in the healthcare industry and a teacher is in the education industry.

Researching your industry will keep you on top of the changes that take place so that you are always offering the most current and up to date products and services. For Mason and his Magic Suds, he will want to know the latest industry standards for dog grooming, like how products may help or harm the environment, or the quality of the ingredients in the formula. If his customers are not aware about new trends in the market, when Mason tells them about it, he will look like a rockstar and an industry expert for introducing clients to the latest and greatest!

What is an industry?

What's an example of staying aware of the changes in your industry?

Staying aware of the changes in your industry simply means paying attention to what's happening around you and your market so that your business stays fresh, creative, and ahead of the game.

Researching Your Competitors

In addition to informing you about changes in your industry, research will also help you keep up with your competitors. Researching your competitors is a cheat code to helping your product or service be the best it can be, while appealing to more potential customers.

That's because you'll come to understand what your competitors do well, what they're not so good at, and how you can address customer needs that they can't or aren't addressing. That's your chance to stand out in the marketplace.

Knowing who and what your business is up against will help you level up your ideas and make your business stronger.

But don't let research scare you! It doesn't have to involve spending tons of hours on the computer, or reading long, boring articles. Research can be an adventure (like the one you're on right now!)

You can get up, go outside, and discover answers in the real world.

Be a Business Detective!

Think of yourself as a detective for your business. Instead of looking for clues to solve a mystery, you're looking for clues about what people want.

🔍

For Mason, that might mean visiting dog parks around the community and asking owners simple questions, like:

"What do you wish your dog's shampoo did better?"

"What scents do you like for your dog?"

These answers help you improve your product and understand your customer better.

Suddenly, research becomes a fun adventure that ends with gathering ideas that help make your product or service even better.

The best part?

Real-world research helps you create something people actually want. And when you know what your customers care about, your message and your marketing become stronger, smarter, and way more successful.

Moment

You now know about your message, and who to give your message to, now let's discover when you should deliver that message.

Timing, or moment, is everything in marketing!

Let's consider:

Is a quiet Tuesday in your neighborhood the best time to market your popcorn business? Maybe—but only if lots of popcorn-loving people are actually around, and because it's quiet, it doesn't seem like there will be. Now consider a packed youth baseball game. That's a way better moment to share your message.

More people, more time, and more hungry fans means a much bigger chance they'll want your delicious popcorn while they watch the game.

⚾
Youth Baseball Game
Great Moment for Popcorn!
Packed crowd + hungry fans = more sales
🏠
Quiet Tuesday at Home
Not the Best Moment
Few people, low energy, less buying mood

How about Mason? Do you think he can market his shampoos year round? Maybe, but perhaps his market would receive his message better in times where their dogs' hair gets tangled most often. Dog hair gets tangled much faster in times of high humidity. So his message sent to his market during times of high humidity — like the summertime — might be received better.

Identifying when the BEST time is to present your message to your market — that is your moment. And for this reason, use everything you know about your product or service to maximize your moment!

That means, consider all of the ways you can best appeal to your market so that they can receive your message. That usually equals a sale!

Maximizing Your Moment

Identifying when the BEST time is to present your message to your market — that is your moment. And for this reason, use everything you know about your product or service to maximize your moment!

Think about Black Friday...

Businesses develop cool messages and discounted prices to best appeal to their market to influence buying when they are buying gifts for Christmas.
